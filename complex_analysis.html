Complex Analysis

1. Complex Numbers and Their Properties

Complex numbers are numbers that have both a real part and an imaginary part. They are usually written in the form a + bi, where a and b are real numbers, and i is the imaginary unit with the property i² = -1.

Complex numbers can be represented geometrically in the complex plane, where the x-axis represents the real part and the y-axis represents the imaginary part. This representation is useful for visualizing operations on complex numbers.

Complex numbers can also be represented in polar form, which consists of a magnitude and an angle. This form is particularly useful for multiplication and division of complex numbers.

2. Analytic Functions

An analytic function is a function that is locally given by a convergent power series. These functions are also known as holomorphic functions.

Analytic functions have many important properties, including the fact that they are infinitely differentiable and that their Taylor series converges to the function in a neighborhood of any point where the function is analytic.

Examples of analytic functions include polynomials, exponential functions, and trigonometric functions.

3. Cauchy-Riemann Equations

The Cauchy-Riemann equations are a set of two partial differential equations that provide a necessary and sufficient condition for a function to be analytic.

If a function f(z) = u(x, y) + iv(x, y) is analytic, then the partial derivatives of u and v must satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann equations: ∂u/∂x = ∂v/∂y and ∂u/∂y = -∂v/∂x.

These equations are fundamental in complex analysis and are used to determine whether a given function is analytic.

4. Contour Integration

Contour integration is a method of evaluating integrals of complex functions along a path, or contour, in the complex plane.

This technique is particularly useful for evaluating integrals that are difficult or impossible to compute using standard real-variable methods.

One of the key results in contour integration is Cauchy's integral theorem, which states that the integral of an analytic function around a closed contour is zero.

5. Cauchy's Integral Formula

Cauchy's integral formula is a fundamental result in complex analysis that expresses the value of an analytic function inside a contour in terms of an integral over the contour.

If f(z) is analytic inside and on a simple closed contour C, and a is a point inside C, then f(a) = (1/2πi) ∫(f(z)/(z-a)) dz, where the integral is taken over C.

This formula has many important consequences, including the fact that analytic functions are infinitely differentiable and that their derivatives can be computed using contour integrals.

6. Laurent Series

The Laurent series is a representation of a complex function as a series that includes both positive and negative powers of the variable.

This series is particularly useful for representing functions that have singularities, or points where the function is not analytic.

The Laurent series can be used to classify singularities and to compute residues, which are important in the evaluation of contour integrals.

7. Residue Theorem

The residue theorem is a powerful tool in complex analysis that allows the evaluation of contour integrals by relating them to the residues of the integrand at its singularities.

If f(z) is analytic inside and on a simple closed contour C, except for a finite number of singularities, then the integral of f(z) around C is 2πi times the sum of the residues of f(z) at its singularities inside C.

This theorem is widely used in the evaluation of integrals and in the solution of problems in physics and engineering.

8. Conformal Mapping

A conformal mapping is a function that preserves angles and the shapes of infinitesimally small figures.

Conformal mappings are used to transform complex functions and to solve problems in complex analysis and applied mathematics.

One of the most important conformal mappings is the Riemann mapping theorem, which states that any simply connected, non-empty open subset of the complex plane can be mapped conformally onto the unit disk.

9. Riemann Surfaces

A Riemann surface is a one-dimensional complex manifold that provides a natural setting for the study of complex functions.

Riemann surfaces are used to extend the domain of complex functions and to study their properties in a more general context.

Examples of Riemann surfaces include the complex plane, the Riemann sphere, and the torus.

10. Analytic Continuation

Analytic continuation is a method of extending the domain of an analytic function beyond its original region of definition.

This technique is used to define functions on larger domains and to study their properties in a more general context.

Analytic continuation is widely used in complex analysis and in the study of special functions, such as the gamma function and the zeta function.
